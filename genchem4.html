Ion Product of Water

The property of water to be amphoteric causes its interaction with another water molecule to undergo auto-ionization. This effect happens when water molecules exchange their protons with one another and it only happens at a very small extent. In this case, one molecule of water act as a Bronsted-Lowry acid (proton donor) while the other water molecule is the Bronsted-Lowry base (proton acceptor). To show the process, the chemical equation for it is:

H2O(l) + H2O(l) ⇌ H3O+(aq) + OH-(aq), or can be simplified to

2H2O(l) ⇌ H3O+(aq) + OH-(aq)

With that chemical reaction at equilibrium, the equilibrium constant designated as Kw with a value of 1.00 x 10-14 at room temperature (25°C). The equilibrium expression is:

Kw= [H3O+][OH-]= 1.00 x 10-14

Solving for the concentrations of hydronium (H3O+) and hydroxide (OH-) ions, we have:

1.00 x 10-14  =  [H3O+][OH-]   =   x2

x= √(1.00 x 10-14)   =   1.00 x 10-7

[H3O+]   =   [OH-]   =   1.00 x 10-7 M

It shows that the dissociated water molecules is very small and it is only applicable at the state of pure water molecule. Changes happen when [H3O+] > [OH-] by which the solution is acidic while [H3O+] < [OH-], the solution is basic. If the temperature changes, like other equilibrium constants, the value changes for auto-ionization of water. Kw ranges from 1.15 x 10-15 at 0°C to 4.99 x 10-13 at 100°C.

Acid-Base Equilibria

Acids and bases have their own strength variety that signifies how much they are capable to donate or accept protons. For strong acids and strong bases, they are capable of complete ionization and thus their equilibrium constants are relatively large. For weak acids and weak bases, they have their equilibrium constants vary greatly depending also on how much proton they can donate or accept. These constants are relatively small in quantity and represented by KA is for equilibrium constant for acid while KB is equilibrium constant for base. In any acid-base reaction, the equilibrium will favor the reaction that moves the proton to the stronger base. We can illustrate the equilibrium constants of acids and bases as follows:

Hypothetical acid in water equilibrium:

HA   +   H2O ⇌   H3O+   +   A−

KA =
[H3O+][A−] [HA]

Hypothetical base in water equilibrium:

A−   +   H2O ⇌   HA   +   OH−

KB =
[HA][OH−] [A−]

The relationship between KA and KB can be illustrated by multiplying the two expressions:

KAKB =
([H3O+][A−] [HA])([HA][OH−] [A−])

KAKB = [H3O+][OH-]

Remember that: KW = [H3O+][OH-], so:

KAKB= KW

This derived equation can be used to correlate KA and KB such that Kw is given at certain temperatures and also indicates that they are inversely proportional to each other.

Diprotic and Polyprotic Acids and Bases

There are acids that can give more than one proton upon their ionization. Thus, they have two or more equilibrium expressions and constants to illustrate their acidity. It is always the first proton than any of the successive proton easier to be removed and donate to the base. For example, we have carbonic acid (H2CO3) at 25°C which is a diprotic acid.

First proton dissociation:

H2CO3 + H2O ⇌ HCO3− + H3O+

KA1 =
[H3O+][HCO3−] [H2CO3]= 4.45 x 10− 7

Second proton dissociation:

HCO3− + H2O ⇌ CO32− + H3O+

KA2 =
[H3O+][CO32−] [HCO3−]= 4.69 x 10− 11

It shows that the value of KA successively becomes smaller in value as protons get donated. It is become less favorable for the acid to donate the proton as it approaches its form with negative ion. The trend of their values should be KA1 > KA2 > and so on. This step by step donation of proton indicates that the acid molecule becomes less acid when protons are lost. On the other hand, polyprotic bases has the same trend and principle of designating KB values such that bases is for accepting protons. For example, the carbonate ion (CO32−) at 25°C is illustrated:

First proton acceptance:

CO32− + H2O ⇌ HCO3− + OH−

KB1 =
[OH−][HCO3−] [CO32−]= 2.13 x 10− 4

Second proton acceptance:

HCO3− + H2O ⇌ H2CO3 + OH−

KB2 =
[OH−][H2CO3] [HCO3−]= 2.25 x 10− 8

    2. Zinc has two naturally occuring isotopes which are Zn65 (64.9292 amu) and Zn67 (66.9271 amu). If zinc has an average atomic mass of 65.38 amu, what are the percentage abundances of each given isotope? (Topic: Average Atomic Mass)

      3. To solve for the percentage abundance, remember that the overall composition of the atomic mass is 100%. Thus, you can designate the first percentage abundance as x while the second as 1 − x to follow 100%. The solution for this problem is:

      ∑ (%Abundance)(Isotopic Mass) = (x)(64.9292 amu) + (1 − x)(66.9271 amu) = 65.38 amu

      Solving for x, x = 0.7744 and then 1 − x = 1 − 0.7744 = 0.2256

      Therefore, the isotopes have 77.44% Zn65 and 22.56% Zn67


    3. Caustic soda, NaOH, can be prepared commercially by the reaction of Na2CO3 with slaked lime, Ca(OH)2. How many molecules of NaOH can be obtained by treating 1.350 kg of Na2CO3 with Ca(OH)2? (Topic: Avogadro's Number)

      4. Na2CO3   +   Ca(OH)2 → CaCO3   +   2NaOH


      To solve for the molecules of NaOH, use the principle of reaction stoichiometry to achieve moles of NaOH and then use Avogadro's number to obtain the molecules. Initially, convert the given kilograms to grams (note that 1 kg = 1000 grams):

      The given is 1.350 kg Na2CO3 = 1350 g Na2CO3,

      molecules of NaOH = (1350 g Na2CO3)
      (1 mole Na2CO3 106 grams Na2CO3)(2 moles NaOH 1 mole Na2CO3)(6.022 x 1023 molecules NaOH 1 mole NaOH)

      =1.53 x 1025 molecules NaOH


    4. For the reaction of Ba(OH)2 + 2HClO3 → Ba(ClO3)2   +   2H2O, what is the number of moles of H2O formed when 0.200 moles of Ba(OH)2 is treated with 0.500 moles of HClO3? (Topic: Reaction Stoichiometry and Limiting Reactants)


      5. The solution to find the moles of H2O is to determine first which of the following starting materials (Ba(OH)2 and HClO3) is the limiting reactant. You can simply convert each into the moles of H2O:

      moles of H2O from Ba(OH)2= ( 0.200 moles Ba(OH)2)
      (2 moles H2O 1 mole Ba(OH)2) = 0.400 moles
      moles of H2O from HClO3 = ( 0.500 moles HClO3)
      (2 moles H2O 2 moles HClO3) = 0.500 moles

      From the calculation, it shows that Ba(OH)2 is the limiting reactant and the moles of H2O is 0.400 moles.
