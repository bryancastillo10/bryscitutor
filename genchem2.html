Chemical Equations

A chemical equation is the representation of chemists or scientists on what chemical reaction occured by which it could either make or break the bonds between the molecules or compounds involved. By convention, the molecules on the left side which are the starting materials are called the reactants while the molecules on the right side are the molecules formed due to the result of the chemical reaction are called products. In writing chemical equations, the amount of both reactants and products should be balanced as it should follow the fundamental chemical observation of the law of conservation of mass. To balance chemical equations, factors or the numbers before the chemical symbols are used which are callled the coefficients. For example, we have the chemical reaction between carbon monoxide (CO) and oxygen molecule (O2) which forms carbon dioxide (CO2):

CO(g) + O2(g) → CO2(g)

It shows that the amount of oxygen of the reactants as compared to the products is not balanced. To make the equation balanced, add a coefficient or number before the chemical symbols that would yield equal amounts of all the elements involved. In this example, we have carbon and oxygen as the elements to be balanced. The coefficient that fits with the elements is 2 (you can choose any number as long as it can balance the chemical equation, but in this simple chemical equation, coefficient 2 would be the best option). From this examples, add the coefficents 2 to both carbon monoxide (CO) and carbon dioxide (CO2). The equation is now:

2CO(g) + O2(g) → 2CO2(g)

From that chemical equation with coefficients, you can verify your balancing with coefficients by comparing the amount of elements on each side of the reactions. It should be equal to each other, interpretation for thea example is summarized:

2CO(g) O2(g) Total Number in Reactants 2CO2(g) Total Number in Products
Carbon (C) 2 0 2 2 2
Oxygen (O) 2 2 4 4 4

As a result of verification, the total number of carbons from the reactants and products is 2 while oxygen has 4 for both sides. Thus, the whole chemical equation is balanced.

Types of Chemical Reactions

Through chemical reactions, atoms of one or more compounds allows itself to form different chemical compounds. Indicators that a chemical reaction occured can be through temperature changes, explosion, formation of gas, formation of precipitates, or through color change. Those characteristics that were observed by scientists leads them to classify the chemical reactions. There are five common types of chemical reactions that occur within compounds. In general chemistry, these types of reactions are synthesis reaction, decomposition reaction, single displacement reaction, double displacement reaction, and combustion reaction.

  • Synthesis Reaction- two simple compounds combine to form a single compound. The general formula for this reaction can be represented as: A + B → AB.

    • Examples:
    2H2(g) + O2(g) → 2H2O(g)
    2Mg(s) + O2(g) → 2MgO(s)

  • Decomposition Reaction- the inverse of synthesis reaction by which a single compounds breaks into simpler compounds or elements. The general formula for this reaction can be represented as: AB → A + B

    • Examples:
    2H2O(g) → 2H2(g) + O2(g)
    2NH3(g) → N2(g) + H2(g)

  • Single Displacement Reaction-the reaction when one element replaces another in a compound. The reaction is common with metallic elements involved and an activity series of metals was established to show which of the metals is more reactive to undergo single displacement. The general reaction can be represented as: BC + A → B + AC

    • Examples:
    Fe(aq) + CuSO4(aq) → Cu(aq) + FeSO4(aq)
    Cl2(g) + 2KI(aq) → 2KCl(aq) + I2(aq)

  • Double Displacement Reaction-a reaction that occurs when a metal replaces a metal in a compound and a nonmetal in a compound forming two new compounds. The general reaction can be represented as: AB + CD → BC + AD.

    • Examples:
    AgNO3(aq)+ NaCl(aq) → AgCl(s)+ NaNO3(aq)
    MgCl2(aq) + 2KOH → Mg(OH)2(aq) + 2KCl(aq)

  • Combustion Reaction- this reaction involves any hydrocarbon compounds (CxHy) that reacts with oxygen that produces carbon dioxide and water.

    • Examples:
    CH4(g) + O2(g) → CO2(g) + 2H2O(g)
    C5H12(g) + 8O2(g) → 5CO2(g) + 6H2O(g)

Mass and Moles Relationships (Reaction Stoichiometry)

Atoms are extremely small particles by which it cannot be detected by naked eye and it is impossible to weigh every single atoms needed. With that problem, scientists had arrived with a solution to determine the mass of one atom relative to another through experiments. The first step is to assign a value to the mass of one atom of a given element so that it can be used as a standard. By international agreement, atomic mass is the mass of the atom in atomic mass units (amu). One atomic mass unit is defined as a mass exactly equal to one-twelfth the mass of one carbon-12 atom. Carbon-12 is the carbon isotope that has six protons and six neutrons. Setting the atomic mass of carbon-12 at 12 amu provides the standard for measuring the atomic mass of the other elements.

For example, hydrogen atom is known to have 8.40% as massive as the carbon-12 atom. Through that, we can determine the atomic mass of hydrogen by 0.084 multiplied to 12.00 amu which equals to 1.008 amu. Similar calculations can also be shown on nitrogen atom, oxygen atom, and other elements.

Average Atomic Mass

Most of the naturally occuring elements like carbon atom came to have more than one isotopes. Isotopes are pair or group of a single element that has the same number of protons but different number of neutrons. Thus they vary greatly on the atomic mass since it is equal to the number of protons and neutrons of an atom. This means that when we measure the atomic mass of an element, we must generally settle for the average mass of the naturally occurring mixture of isotopes. We can determine the average atomic mass through the atomic masses of each isotopes with respect to its natural abundances. The equation for the determination of average atomic mass can be illustrated as:

Average Atomic Mass= ∑(%Abundance)(Isotopic Mass)


Sample Problem: Silver (Ag) has two naturally occuring isotopes which are 107Ag and 109Ag. Given the following data, calculate the average atomic mass of Ag.

Isotope Mass(amu) % Abundance
107Ag 106.9059 51.84
109Ag 108.90476 48.16

Following the formula, we have:
∑ (%Abundance)(Isotopic Mass) =
(51.84 100) (106.9059) + (48.16 100) (108.90476)= 107.9 amu

Moles and Avogadro's Number

Atomic mass units provide a relative scale for the masses of the elements. But because atoms have such small masses, no usable scale can be devised to weigh them in calibrated units of atomic mass units. In the SI system, the unit mole (mol) is the amount of a substance that contains as many elementary entities as there are atoms in exactly 12 g (or 0.012 kg) of the carbon-12 isotope. These entities could either be atoms, molecules,ions and other particles. Through experiments, scientists derived a constant number which is equivalent to one mole of atoms,molecules, or particles. It is called as the Avogadro's number in honor of the Italian scientist, Amedeo Avogadro.

Na = 6.022 x 1023

Percentage Composition

Percentage composition by mass is the percent by mass of each element in a compound. You can obtain it by dividing the mass of each element in 1 mole of the compound by the molar mass of the compound and then multiplying by 100 percent. It can be mathematically expressed as:

%Composition =
(n x MWelement MWcompound) x 100%

where n is the number of moles of the element to be determined on percentage composition.

Conversion of Mass and Moles

The amounts of reactants and products can be obtained through quantitative interperetation called stoichiometry. This process is a core idea in chemistry in order to transact moles to masses,masses to moles, or masses to masses of reactants and products present in a chemical reaction. Other parameters such as pressures and volumes (for gases) can also be identified through the concept of stoichiometry. To perform this technique, it is also similar to dimensional analysis on units like converting grams to kilograms. In stoichiometry with respect to chemical reactions, the key factors to achieve the desired value are the molecular mass or molecular weight of the elements and the compounds(unit is g/mol), and the mole ratio that is based on a balanced chemical reaction. Limiting Reactants and Percentage Yield

In a chemical reaction, we can ask the question when will the chemical put to a stop? The answer for it is the amount of the limiting reactant. In some chemical reactions, especially synthesis, displacements, and combustion reactions, contain more than one reactants in the chemical reaction. With such reactions, one of them is classified as the limiting reactant by which it can be determined through their amounts. Meanwhile, the remaining reactants are called the excess reactants. You can take the principle to have the analogy as making a delicious brewed coffee. For that, you're going to need brewed coffee beans, creamer, and sugar which represents the reactants. If one of those three is totally consumed or phased out from the supplies, that would stop you in making brewed coffee. Thus, one of them represents as the limiting reactant while the remaining components are the excess reactants. In chemical reactions, limiting reactants can be determined by identifying the amount(either in terms of mass or moles) of each of the reactants and then the smallest amount among the reactants is the limiting reactant. To further visualize the concept, consider the reaction of aluminum (Al) with chlorine gas (Cl2) to form aluminum chloride(AlCl3). For example, there are 10 grams of aluminum and 20 grams of chlorine gas as your starting material for the reaction.

2Al + 3Cl2 → 2AlCl3

Using the balanced chemical equation, determine the grams of AlCl3 for each of the reactants,

For Al:

grams of AlCl3 = (10.0 grams Al)
(1 mole Al 27 grams Al) (2 moles AlCl32 moles Al) ( 133.35 grams AlCl3 1 mole AlCl3)= 49.39 grams AlCl3

For Cl2:

grams of AlCl3 = (20.0 grams Cl2)
(1 mole Cl2 70.9 grams Cl2) (2 moles AlCl33 moles Cl2) ( 133.35 grams AlCl3 1 mole AlCl3)= 25.08 grams AlCl3

Comparing the grams of AlCl3 resulted from the two reactants, the smallest amount from the two results (49.39 grams < 25.08 grams) is from chlorine gas. Thus, the limiting reactant in this example is Cl2. It means that when the whole 20 grams of Cl2 is totally consumed in the chemical reaction, the reaction stops that has a theoretical yield of 25.08 grams of grams AlCl3. Another concept being studied in chemical reactions is the percentage yield which shows a comparison of the theoretical yield with respect to the actual yields. Actual yield is the amount of the product obtained based on an experiment. For example, a certain experiment with the same chemical reaction with aluminum and chlorine gas yields to the production of 23.85 grams of grams AlCl3. What is the percentage yield of AlCl3 reaction such that the starting materials is the same with the first example (10 grams of Al and 20 grams of Cl2)?

We already know that the limiting reactant is Cl2 by which the grams of AlCl3 obtained is the theoretical yield. To solve for percentage yield, take the ratio of the actual yield over the theoretical yield multiplied by 100 percent:

Percentage Yield =
Actual Yield Theoretical Yield x 100% =Actual Yield of grams AlCl3Theoretical Yield of grams AlCl3x 100% = 23.85 grams AlCl3 25.08 grams AlCl3x 100% = 95%

Thus, the percentage yield of the formation of AlCl3 is 95% based on the given starting materials and result of an experiment.


Elemental and Combustion Analysis

Chemical formula are the basis of the components of a chemical reaction. In the laboratory, chemists perform experiments in obtaining the masses of a specific compound and through that, they can derive the chemical formula and identity of a substance. Such analysis is called elemental analysis, by which masses of each elements identified were used to formulate the chemical formula of a substance. Elemental analysis is an application for both the principle of percent by mass and reaction stoichiometry. Empirical formula, the simplest chemical formula with respect to its reduced subscripts, can be obtained from the masses of the each of the elements and converting it to moles. From the moles of the elements, mole ratio can be obtained. However, if you want to obtain the molecular formula which is the actual formula of the compound, a whole number factor should be multiplied to the subscripts of the empirical formula. Molecular Mass (MM) Empirical Mass (EM)

Another type of analysis with the application of reaction stoichiometry and percentage by mass is combustion analysis. Combustion analysis mainly focuses on the identification of the chemical formula for hydrocarbons and alcohols. These are identified with the basis of elemental analysis on the usual products of combustion reactions. The amount of carbon dioxide is relative to the amount of carbon while the amount of water is relative to the amount of hydrogen in the hydrocarbon. We can illustrate the typical combustion reaction as:

CxHy + O2 → CO2 + H2O

Mole ratios for the stoichiometry is: 1 mol CO2= 1 mol C, and 1 mol H2O = 2 mol H

If the hydrocarbon contains oxygen (O) which is an alcohol and the mass of oxygen is unknown such that the molecular mass of the alcohol is given. Here is the strategy to solve for the empirical formula:

  1. Identify the libreated H2O in order to find the total H2O content. After that the moles of H2O and Na2SO4 is proportional to the coefficients in its chemical formula. The process is:

    2. H2Oliberated= 1.500 grams sample - 0.685 grams dried= 0.815 grams H2O

    0.815 grams H2O = (90%)(H2Ototal)

    H2Ototal= 0.815 grams/0.90 = 0.906 grams H2O

    Assume that the sample is 100% dry to solve for grams of Na2SO4: = 1.500 grams sample - 0.906 grams H2O = 0.594 grams Na2SO4

    For moles of H2O and Na2SO4:

    moles of H2O = (0.906 g H2O)
    (1 mole H2O 18 g H2O)= 0.0503 mol H2O
    moles of Na2SO4 = (0.594 g Na2SO4)
    (1 mole Na2SO4 142 g Na2SO4)= 4.18 x 10-3 mol Na2SO4

    By ratio and proportion of moles to coefficients:

    ( 4.18 x 10-3 mol Na2SO4 0.0503 mol H2O)= ( 1 mol Na2SO4 x )

    x = 12

  2. To solve chemical formula in elemental analysis, convert the given grams to moles and then solve for mole ratio. For mole ratio, identify the smallest moles among the elements and use it as the factor to divide each of the elements. The solution can be summarized in a table as shown:
    3. Elements Mass Moles Mole Ratio
    Na 2.82 grams 0.1226 moles 1
    Cl 4.35 grams 0.1227 moles 1
    O 7.83 grams 0.4894 moles 4
    From the table, you can observe that grams are converted into moles (first and second column). It is through the use molecular weights of the elements, for Na=23 g/mol, for Cl=35.45 g/mol ,and O = 18 g/mol. After the moles were identified, the result shows that 0.1226 moles is the least value among the moles. This value is to be used to determine the mole ratio, dividing each of the moles, you obtain the mole ratio (column 3). As a result, the mole ratio are 1:1:4 which concludes as the empirical formula of NaClO4.

  3. Same process like in problem number 2 goes through with combustion analysis in finding empirical formula. The difference is that the given is in % by mass instead of masses. Assume that the components are in 100% total and proportional to each grams. It means that 40% carbon is 40 grams carbon and so on. The solution is:

    4. Elements % by Mass Mass Moles Mole Ratio

    H 6.71 6.71 grams 6.71 moles 2
    O 53.33 53.33 grams 3.33 moles 1
    The mole ratio is 1:2:1, thus the empirical formula is CH2O. Next is to identify the factor for molecular formula identification. The molecular mass is given in the problem (90.08 g/mol) while empirical mass can be solved on the molecular weight of the empirical formula which is 30 g/mol. Solving for the factor:
    n =
    Molecular Mass (MM) Empirical Mass (EM)=90.08 g/mol 30 g/mol= 3

    The factor is 3, multiplying it to the subscripts of the empirical formula, we have: 3 · CH2O = C3H6O3 as the molecular formula of the unknown alcohol.


In some instances, when dividing the moles with the smallest moles, the ratio to be obtained is not an integer (not an integer is like 0.25 or 0.33). Rounding off the value to the nearest whole number is not a valid solution to obtain the mole ratio. The solution is to multiply the result of mole ratio with a whole number that could make it an integer. Upon multiplying with a number, all other elements even though it yields a whole number, would be affected with the multiplication of that factor. For example, the resulting mole ratio upon division gives a result of 2.33. Mole ratio 2.33 is not valid to be rounded off so it should be multiplied by a number, let's say 3 to have a value of 6.99 or approximately 7. All other elements should be multiplied by 3 to make it uniform on the empirical formula. If you encountered, these instances in solving chemical formula. Mole ratio with decimal points 0.25 should be multipled by 4,   0.33 should be multipled by 3,   0.50 should be multipled by 2,   0.67 should be multipled by 3,   and 0.75 should be multipled by 4 in order to make them a whole number.
