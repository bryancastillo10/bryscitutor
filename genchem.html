Introduction to General Chemistry Course

Chemistry is the study of matter and the chemical reactions between the substances. Matter is defined as anything that occupies space and has mass. To further understand chemistry, here are basic terminologies:

  • Atom- indivisible particles that were hypothesized by Democritus to comprise in order to form an element.
    Also known as the building blocks of matter. An atom is generally composed of smaller particles namely proton, electron, and neutron.
  • Proton- positively charged particles that is located in the nucleus of an atom
  • Electron- negatively charged particles that is outside the nucleus of an atom
  • Neutron- neutral particles that is associated with proton in the nucleus of an atom
  • Nucleon- collective term for protons and neutron
  • Element- a substance that cannot be broken down by simple chemical changes
  • Compound- the substance formed when two or more elements are chemically bonded together
  • Mixture- the combination of two or more substance without any chemical means, no chemical reactions take place with their formation
  • Homogeneous Mixture- a component of solid, liquid, and gaseous mixture that has the same proportions of each components
  • Heterogeneous Mixture- the opposite of homogeneous mixture by which its components vary in proportions

Classifications of Matter

States of Matter

There are three fundamental states of matter namely, solid, liquid, and gas. These states generally differs on the distances between the molecules in order to occupy its space. In a solid, molecules are held close together in an orderly fashion with little freedom of motion. Molecules in a liquid are close together but are not held so rigidly in position and can move past one another. In a gas, the molecules are separated by distances that are large compared with the size of the molecules. The three states of matter can be interconverted without changing the composition of the substance. This process is called phase change. For example, ice turns into liquid water by melting. It can be expressed as: H2O(s)⇌H2O(l).


An ice that melts is an intensive property. The color of the zinc oxide which is white is an extensive property. The flammability of gasoline as it burns through combustion reaction is a chemical property.

Properties of Matter

Matters have classifications depending on its properties. There are physical properties that could either be observed or tasted. It can be subclassified dependinf on the amount dependence of the substance. Another classification is the its chemical properties which describes the property of a substance to undergo chemical reaction forming a new substance.

  • Extensive Property- quantity dependent property, the value of these properties changes with respect to the amount of matter/substance

    • Examples: Volume, Mass, Size, Weight, Length

  • Intensive Property- quantity independent property, the value of these properties does not change with respect to the amount of matter of matter/substance

    • Examples: Boiling Point, Temperature, Color, Luster,Hardness

  • Chemical Property- property that is exhibited by matter as it undergoes changes in composition or chemical reactions

    • Examples: Flammability, Reactivity, Oxidation States, Enthalpy


A Brief History on the Discovery of Atoms and Atomic Sub-Particles

An atom is generally composed of protons,electrons, and neutrons by which were discovered by different scientists with their respective experiments. In the early fifth century, the Greek philosopher Democritus expressed his belief that all matter consists of very small, indivisible particles by which he called atomos (meaning uncuttable or indivisible). At some point, Democritusâ€™ idea was not generally accepted by many people. Until experimental evidences from early scientific investigations provided support for that belief and gradually gave rise to the modern definitions of elements and compounds. An English scientist, John Dalton, established hypotheses about the nature of atoms that gave also birth to the modern era of chemistry. The following are Dalton's atomic theory:

  • Elements are composed of extremely small particles called atoms.
  • All atoms of a given element are identical, having the same size, mass, and chemical properties. The atoms of one element are different from the atoms of all other elements.
  • Compounds are composed of atoms of more than one element. In any compound, the ratio of the numbers of atoms of any two of the elements present is either an integer or a simple fraction.
  • A chemical reaction involves only the separation, combination, or rearrangement of atoms; it does not result in their creation or destruction.

Through that theories derived by Dalton, we can now define atom as the basic unit of an element that can enter into chemical combination. A series of researches were followed regarding the sub-atomic particles involved in the composition of an atom. A brief summary is shown below:

Discovery Proponent/s Experiment Principle
Proton Ernest Rutherford Gold Foil Experiment Rutherford shots a beam of alpha particles in a sheet of gold foil, a few particles were deflected. He concludes that a tiny, dense particles causes these deflections.
Electron J.J. Thomson Cathode Ray Tube Experiment Thomson placed two-oppositely charged electrical plates around a cathode ray. The ray formed were deflected away from the negatively-charged plate towards the positively-charged plate. He concluded that the ray is composed of negatively charged particles.

At constant rate, the force of gravity on the drop and the force of electrical field on the drop are equal. The charge was calculated based on the mechanical equilibrium demonstrated by the experiment.
Neutron James Chadwick Berillyium with α-particle experiment Chadwick bombarded alpha-particles into a beryllium which emits a very energetic stream of radiation. These rays were penetrating and not deflected in a magnetic field which concludes these particles to be neutral.

Fundamental Chemical Observations

The idea of Dalton's theories on the nature of atom also gaves rise to the laws that clearly describes the behavior of an atom with its interaction to other atoms. Those laws are the law of definite proportions established by Joseph Proust, a French chemist; the law of multiple proportions; and the law of conservation of mass. These laws strongly supports the hypotheses of John Dalton.

  • Law of Definite Proportions-states that different samples of the same compound always contain its constituent elements in the same proportion by mass. For example,if we were to analyze samples of carbon dioxide gas obtained from different sources, we would find in each sample the same ratio by mass of carbon to oxygen.
  • Law of Multiple Proportions-states that if two elements can combine to form more than one compound, the masses of one element that combine with a fi xed mass of the other element are in ratios of small whole numbers. For example, carbon forms two stable compounds with oxygen, namely, carbon monoxide and carbon dioxide. This best explains the principle of balancing chemical equations.
  • Law of Conservation of Mass-states that matter can be neither created nor destroyed. It is because matter is made of atoms that are unchanged in a chemical reaction, it follows that mass must be conserved as well. This principle is mainly used by chemical engineers to design chemical processes in a large scale.

The Periodic Table of Elements

Throughout the discovery of elements and their chemical properties, recognition of periodic regularities in physical and chemical behavior are in need to organize the large volume of available information. It led to the development of the periodic table of elements, a chart in which elements having similar chemical and physical properties are grouped together. It was first proposed by Dmitri Mendeleev, a Russian chemist last 1869.


The Periodic Table of Elements

Notation on Elements

All atoms can be identified by the number of protons and neutrons they contain. The atomic number (Z) is the number of protons in the nucleus of each atom of an element. In a neutral atom the number of protons is equal to the number of electrons, so the atomic number also indicates the number of electrons present in the atom. The chemical identity of an atom can be determined solely from its atomic number. Meanwhile, the mass number (A) is the total number of neutrons and protons present in the nucleus of an atom of an element. It has exception for the most common form of hydrogen, which has one proton and no neutrons. The number of neutrons in an atom is equal to the difference between the mass number and the atomic number, or A minus Z.

A is the mass number, Z is the atomic number, i is the charge of the ion if the element is not neutral

Molecules and Chemical Formula

A molecule is an aggregate of at least two atoms in a definite arrangement held together by chemical forces (also called chemical bonds ). A molecule may contain atoms of the same element or atoms of two or more elements joined in a fixed ratio as stated by the law of definite proportions. Molecules are commonly expressed through chemical formula that allows to show the compositions in terms of the chemical symbols. Chemical formula can be categorized into two: molecular formula and empirical formula.

  1. Molecular Formula shows the actual number of atoms of each element in the smallest unit of a substance.
  2. Empirical Formula tells us which elements are present and the simplest whole-number ratio of their atoms. However, it does not necessarily the actual number of atoms in a given molecule.

An effective way to represent molecules or compounds are through molecular models and chemical formula. As of now, there are over 20 million known chemical compounds that results for chemists to establish a nomenclature or rules in naming chemical compounds. It greatly depends on the structure and how the bonds of each elements involved in the compound were bonded.
